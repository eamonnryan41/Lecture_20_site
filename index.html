Eamonn Ryan: Batted Ball Data

Just me today

Motivation

Every year, baseball statistics become more and more advanced as teams try to find a competitive advantage over their opponents. The quest for succcess has led to the collection of data at almost every turn of a baseball game, with the most important statistics deriving from the most important part of the game: hitting the ball. Some of the most relevant and discussed statistics in the game today are exit velocity (avg_hit_speed) and distance. Distance is obviously important because it translate to balls going further, which translates to a greater chance for a batted ball to become home run or a ball that can't be caught by the opposing team. For a sports fan and (hopefully) future sports journalist, all this data is exactly what I'd be working with to evaluate talent and compare it to production on the field. These are also scratching the surface of baseball statistics â€“ this data is solely based on what the batter does to the ball and has no relation to the result (out, hit, or something else) of the batted ball.

Data

The dataset I chose has all of these statistics from the 2022 season and was downloaded as a .csv file from baseballsavant.mlb.com at this link: https://baseballsavant.mlb.com/leaderboard/statcast. It had the names of players, times they made contact with the ball, their hit angle, sweet spot percentage, hit speed, distance, and number of times they barreled the ball (hit it hard). There was some cleaning up that needed to happen. The "first_names" column had a space before it, so I had to change that before concatenating the two for my tooltip.

Task Analysis

When exploring this data, what I wanted to search for was correlation. What is a good predictor of success? Do higher average distances correlate with higher maximum distances? Which players can hit the ball both far and quickly? There was plenty of exploring to do.

Data Analysis

I found there were some correlations between a couple of statistics, and I could present them. For example, my first graph concerns the correlation between hit speed and distance. Speed and angle (as well as wind resistance but that isn't measured) should physically determine the distance, but what if we isolated one of them? After I isolated speed and saw a slight correlation, I wanted to see if that had any impact on the furthest ball a player hit.

Design Process

In this process, I was focused mostly on making a visualization that would showcase the connection between variables such as hit speed and distance, and also show individual players' performances. This was going to be accomplished using a selection tool, which I implemented into my first visualization. It was difficult to make the maximum distance easily viewable with so many players in this dataset, so I just had to use a tooltip instead after changing the color of the bar graph based on the selection. For the second chart, I wanted to do something I hadn't really considered before, so the correlogram made sense with all these comparitive statistics I was using.

Data Visualizations

First Visualization

For this chart, I explored each individual batter's hit speed and distance, and then used a multi selection tool to compare the maximum distance of a batted ball by the batter. I also used a tooltip to show all the stats for that batter and what their name was, allowing the user to more easily compare the maximum distance in the graph on the right. I also encoded color into the graph so the selection tool would show which data point the user has selected. In terms of tasks, I was looking to make this an exploratory graph that allows for comparison, which is why the tooltip, filtering, and multi selection tool were all included. Allowing users to find players they might know is also helpful for exploration and looking up where someone is placed. Some initial takeaways from this one are that there appears to be a correlation between hit speed and distance, as there is a slight positive trend, but someone who has a great average distance and speed doesn't necessarily have a great maximum distance compared to his opponents.

Second Visualization

In this visualization, I wanted to compare a bunch of different scatter plots across a matrix to see which values had strong correlations. I was most interested in the first column, as average distance is the y value and that can be very important for baseball teams as they evaluate talent and look for a competitive advantage, but looking at brl_percent (barrel percentage) is also helpful. I chose to use the multi selection and tooltip again for the purposes of exploration of this data, as it would be fun to take a look at how certain players fit in across the spectrum. Color was once again encoded for the selection portion to show the individual player's statistics and how they fit into the whole.
